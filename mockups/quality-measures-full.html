CMS quality measures for all long-stay and short-stay residents — includes indicators specifically designed to flag dementia care concerns.

Elevated Antipsychotic Use

This facility medicates 28.3% of long-stay residents with antipsychotic drugs — nearly double the national average of 14.9%. High rates may indicate the facility is using medication to manage dementia behavior rather than providing person-centered care.

Antipsychotic medication
28.3% vs 14.9% avg
What this means: 28.3% of long-stay residents receive antipsychotic medication without a qualifying diagnosis (schizophrenia, Huntington's, Tourette's are excluded by CMS). High rates often indicate chemical restraint of dementia patients rather than appropriate behavioral interventions.
Physical restraints
0.0% vs 0.4% avg
What this means: No residents are physically restrained. CMS has driven restraint use to near-zero nationally. Any facility above 1% should raise serious questions about care practices.
Falls with major injury
4.1% vs 3.4% avg
What this means: 4.1% of long-stay residents experienced a fall resulting in fracture, head injury, or other major harm. Dementia patients are at higher fall risk. Rates above average may indicate inadequate supervision or fall prevention.
Depressive symptoms
18.2% vs 22.0% avg
What this means: 18.2% of residents show moderate to severe depressive symptoms — below average. A lower rate can indicate better screening and treatment, though very low rates may also reflect under-reporting. Consider alongside other indicators.
Significant weight loss
9.8% vs 7.1% avg
What this means: 9.8% of residents experienced significant unplanned weight loss. Dementia patients often have difficulty eating or forget meals. Elevated rates can indicate inadequate feeding assistance. Very low rates could also mean the facility isn't weighing residents regularly.

Mixed Safety Indicators

This facility has above-average pressure ulcer and UTI rates but acceptable catheter use and pain management. Elevated bedsores and infections often correlate with understaffing.

Pressure ulcers (bedsores)
8.7% vs 5.2% avg
What this means: 8.7% of high-risk long-stay residents have stage 2 or worse pressure ulcers (bedsores). Bedsores are largely preventable with proper repositioning, nutrition, and skin checks. A rate this high suggests residents aren't being turned and repositioned frequently enough — often a direct result of insufficient nursing staff.
Urinary tract infections
4.8% vs 3.1% avg
What this means: 4.8% of long-stay residents have a UTI. UTIs are often preventable with proper hygiene care and hydration. Elevated rates can indicate residents aren't being changed frequently enough or aren't getting adequate fluids — both staffing-dependent care activities.
Catheter use
1.2% vs 1.8% avg
What this means: 1.2% of long-stay residents have an indwelling urinary catheter. Catheters significantly increase infection risk and should only be used when medically necessary. Lower is better. Some facilities leave catheters in because it requires less staff time than assisting residents to the bathroom.
Moderate or severe pain
6.4% vs 8.3% avg
What this means: 6.4% of long-stay residents report moderate to severe pain. Good pain management is a basic quality indicator. However, very low rates in facilities with many dementia patients may indicate under-detection — residents with cognitive impairment may not be able to report pain accurately.
Ability to move independently declined
18.9% vs 15.2% avg
What this means: 18.9% of long-stay residents lost the ability to move around independently. Some decline is expected with aging, but above-average rates may indicate the facility isn't providing enough physical therapy or encouraging mobility — which accelerates decline.
Lower is better for all indicators in this section

High Re-hospitalization Rate

This facility sends 24.1% of short-stay patients back to the hospital within 30 days — significantly above the 17.3% national average. If you're choosing a facility for post-surgical rehab, this is a critical warning sign.

Re-hospitalized within 30 days
24.1% vs 17.3% avg
What this means: Nearly 1 in 4 short-stay patients at this facility end up back in the hospital within 30 days. This is the single most important measure for rehab patients. High rates often indicate poor post-operative care, medication errors, or inadequate clinical monitoring. Hospital discharge planners track this metric closely.
Emergency room visits
14.2% vs 10.8% avg
What this means: 14.2% of short-stay patients required an ER visit. Combined with a high re-hospitalization rate, this suggests the facility struggles to manage post-acute complications on site. Facilities with strong clinical teams can handle more issues without sending patients to the emergency room.
Functional improvement
62.4% vs 74.1% avg
What this means: Only 62.4% of short-stay patients showed improvement in daily activities (eating, bathing, walking) — compared to 74.1% nationally. The whole point of rehab is to get better. A below-average improvement rate suggests the therapy program may be inadequate.
New antipsychotic medication
3.1% vs 1.8% avg
What this means: 3.1% of short-stay patients were newly started on antipsychotic medication during their stay. If your family member entered for rehab and came out on a new antipsychotic, that's a red flag. These drugs have serious side effects for elderly patients including increased fall risk and mortality.
Short-stay pressure ulcers
1.2% vs 1.6% avg
What this means: 1.2% of short-stay patients developed new or worsening pressure ulcers during their stay. Developing a bedsore during a rehab stay is a sign of poor basic nursing care — it should never happen during a short stay.
Lower is better for all indicators except Functional Improvement (higher is better)
Quality measures are derived from the CMS Minimum Data Set (MDS), reported quarterly for all Medicare-certified nursing facilities. Antipsychotic rates exclude residents with schizophrenia, Huntington's disease, and Tourette syndrome per CMS methodology. These indicators highlight patterns — a single elevated measure does not automatically indicate poor care but warrants further investigation. Methodology · Verify on CMS.gov →

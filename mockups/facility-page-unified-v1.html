05 How Are Residents Doing?New 7 CMS SOURCES
34 quality measures across 6 categories — real data from MDS assessments, claims, SNF QRP, and VBP programs

High Chemical Sedation

28.3% of long-stay residents on antipsychotics — nearly double the 16.7% national average. Combined with above-average sedative use, this suggests chemical restraints may be used in place of proper behavioral care.

Antipsychotic medication
28.3%vs16.7%
What this means: 28.3% of long-stay residents receive antipsychotic medications — drugs designed for schizophrenia and bipolar disorder but frequently used to sedate dementia patients. The national average is 16.7%. The FDA has a black-box warning against antipsychotic use in elderly dementia patients due to increased death risk. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 419)
Anti-anxiety & sedative medication
24.1%vs19.6%
What this means: 24.1% of residents receive anti-anxiety or hypnotic medications. These include benzodiazepines (Xanax, Ativan, Valium) which increase fall risk in the elderly by 40-60%. The national average is 19.6%. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 425)
Physical restraints
0.0%vs0.1%
What this means: No residents are physically restrained (belts, vests, wrist ties, bed rails used as restraints). This is good — physical restraints cause injury, pressure sores, and psychological trauma. Federal law requires facilities to be restraint-free except in emergencies. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 421)
Depressive symptoms
8.2%vs11.4%
What this means: 8.2% of residents show signs of depression based on standardized screening. Below the 11.4% national average. However, depression is often underdetected in nursing homes — low numbers can mean good care or poor screening. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 402)
Falls with major injury
4.2%vs3.3%
What this means: 4.2% of long-stay residents experienced a fall resulting in a major injury (fractures, head trauma, dislocations). National average is 3.3%. Falls are the leading cause of injury death in adults 65+. High rates often correlate with understaffing and sedative medication use. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 431)
↓ Lower is better for all measures in this category

Elevated Bedsore & UTI Rates

Above-average pressure ulcers and urinary tract infections — the two clearest indicators that residents aren't getting enough hands-on care.

Pressure ulcers (bedsores)
8.7%vs5.1%
What this means: 8.7% of long-stay residents developed new or worsening pressure ulcers (bedsores). National average is 5.1%. Bedsores are almost entirely preventable with proper repositioning every 2 hours. High rates are a hallmark of understaffing — there aren't enough aides to turn residents regularly. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 403)
Urinary tract infections
3.4%vs1.7%
What this means: 3.4% of residents have urinary tract infections. National average is 1.7%. UTIs in nursing homes are often caused by prolonged catheter use, poor hygiene, or delayed incontinence care. For elderly residents, UTIs can cause delirium, falls, and hospitalization. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 424)
Catheter left in
0.8%vs1.0%
What this means: 0.8% of residents have an indwelling urinary catheter. Below the 1.0% national average. Catheters should be removed as soon as medically possible — every day a catheter stays in increases infection risk by 3-7%. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 409)
Significant weight loss
7.8%vs5.4%
What this means: 7.8% of residents experienced unintentional weight loss of 5% or more in 30 days (or 10% in 180 days). National average is 5.4%. Unexplained weight loss in nursing home residents can indicate inadequate feeding assistance, depression, or underlying neglect. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 426)
Ability to do daily tasks declined
18.2%vs14.9%
What this means: 18.2% of residents lost the ability to perform basic activities of daily living (dressing, eating, toileting, bathing). National average is 14.9%. Functional decline in nursing homes is often accelerated by "doing for" rather than "doing with" — staff complete tasks for residents instead of helping them maintain independence. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 406)
Walking ability worsened
19.1%vs16.3%
What this means: 19.1% of residents who could walk experienced a decline in mobility. National average is 16.3%. Once a nursing home resident stops walking, recovery is rare. Facilities should be actively maintaining mobility through walking programs and physical therapy. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 405)
New/worsened incontinence
17.4%vs19.8%
What this means: 17.4% of residents developed new or worsened bladder/bowel control. Below the 19.8% national average. Incontinence management requires regular toileting schedules — a direct reflection of staffing adequacy. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 407)
↓ Lower is better for all measures in this category

High Re-hospitalization Rate

23.9% of short-stay patients return to the hospital within 30 days. If you're choosing a rehab facility after surgery, this is the number that matters most.

Re-hospitalized within 30 days
23.9%vs17.3%
What this means: 23.9% of patients admitted for short-term rehab end up back in the hospital within 30 days. The national average is 17.3%. This is a Medicare claims-based measure — it tracks actual hospital readmissions, not self-reported data. High rates suggest inadequate post-acute care. Source: Claims-Based Quality Measures (Code 471)
Emergency room visits
12.0%vs10.8%
What this means: 12.0% of short-stay residents visited the emergency room. National average is 10.8%. ER visits during a skilled nursing stay often indicate a deterioration in condition that the facility couldn't manage on-site. Source: Claims-Based Quality Measures (Code 470)
Discharged to community
44.2%vs51.4%
What this means: 44.2% of short-stay residents were successfully discharged back to the community (home or assisted living). National average is 51.4%. A lower rate means fewer patients fully recover from their rehab stay. For this measure, higher is better. Source: Claims-Based Quality Measures (Code 451)
Newly started on antipsychotics
3.8%vs1.6%
What this means: 3.8% of short-stay residents were newly put on antipsychotic medications they weren't taking before admission. National average is 1.6%. Starting antipsychotics during a rehab stay — when the patient is supposed to recover and go home — is a red flag for chemical sedation. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 452)
Healthcare-associated infections
0.094vs0.073
What this means: A risk-standardized infection rate of 0.094 vs the 0.073 national average. This measures infections patients acquire during their nursing home stay — including UTIs, pneumonia, and wound infections. These are infections the facility caused or failed to prevent. Source: Claims-Based Quality Measures (Code 486)
Medicare spending per beneficiary
1.14xvs1.0x avg
What this means: Medicare spends 14% more per patient at this facility compared to the national average. Higher spending doesn't mean better care — it often reflects unnecessary hospital readmissions, longer stays, or complications that require additional treatment. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_006)
Functional improvement at discharge
88.7%vs94.3%
What this means: 88.7% of short-stay patients showed functional improvement (mobility, self-care) at discharge. National average is 94.3%. This is the core purpose of rehab — if patients aren't improving, the facility isn't doing its job. For this measure, higher is better. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_007)
Falls with major injury (short-stay)
3.8%vs2.6%
What this means: 3.8% of short-stay patients experienced a fall resulting in major injury during their rehab stay. National average is 2.6%. Rehab patients are especially fall-prone due to post-surgical weakness and new medications. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_038)
CMS payment adjustment
-1.2%penalty78.6% penalized
What this means: CMS cut this facility's Medicare reimbursement by 1.2% based on quality performance under the Value-Based Purchasing (VBP) program. 78.6% of all nursing homes are penalized. The adjustment is based on hospital readmission rates — facilities that send patients back to the hospital too often get paid less. Source: SNF VBP (Incentive Payment Multiplier)
↓ Lower is better for most measures · "Discharged to community" and "Functional improvement" = higher is better

Slow to Fix Problems & Repeat Offender

Takes 48 days on average to correct deficiencies — 50% longer than the national average. Has been cited for the same violations across multiple inspections.

Immediate jeopardy citations
2vs0.3 avg
What this means: 2 citations at the "Immediate Jeopardy" severity level — the highest possible. This means inspectors found conditions that caused or were likely to cause serious injury, harm, impairment, or death. The average facility has 0.3. Only 6.4% of facilities receive any IJ citation. Source: CMS Health Deficiencies
Total fines (last 3 years)
$845,000vs$108,000 avg
What this means: $845,000 in federal penalties over the last 3 years — nearly 8x the national average of $108,000 among penalized facilities. CMS imposes fines for serious deficiencies. Large fines indicate repeated or severe violations. Source: CMS Penalties
Infection control inspections
4vs1.8 avg
What this means: 4 infection control citations in the last 3 years vs the 1.8 national average. Infection control includes hand hygiene, PPE use, isolation protocols, and cleaning procedures. Post-COVID, these inspections are taken more seriously. Source: CMS Health Deficiencies (F880-F882)
Avg days to correct deficiencies
48 daysvs32 avg
What this means: When inspectors find problems, this facility takes an average of 48 days to fix them — 50% longer than the 32-day national average. Slow correction means residents live with hazardous conditions longer. Some deficiencies (blocked exits, medication errors) should be fixed in hours, not weeks. Source: CMS Health Deficiencies (Survey Date vs Correction Date)
Repeat offender citations
7vs3.2 avg
What this means: 7 F-tags have been cited in multiple consecutive inspection cycles — meaning the facility was told to fix the same problem and didn't. National average is 3.2 repeat F-tags. Repeat citations are the strongest indicator that a facility doesn't take corrections seriously. Source: CMS Health Deficiencies (same F-tag, multiple survey dates)
↓ Lower is better for all measures in this category

High Staff Turnover & Weekend Staffing Drop

This facility has a 62.3% annual nursing staff turnover rate — meaning most of the nursing staff leaves within a year. Continuity of care suffers when the people caring for your loved one are constantly changing.

Nursing staff turnover rate
62.3%vs47.7%
What this means: 62.3% of nursing staff (RNs, LPNs, CNAs) left in the last year. National average is 47.7%. New staff don't know residents' routines, medications, or behavioral patterns. Studies show that every 10% increase in turnover is associated with measurable declines in quality indicators. Source: SNF VBP (Provider Info)
Weekend staffing drop
-38%weekday → weekend
What this means: Staffing drops 38% on weekends compared to weekdays. Residents need the same care on Saturday as they do on Tuesday. Weekend staffing cuts mean fewer people to answer call lights, administer medications, reposition bedridden residents, and respond to emergencies. Falls, missed medications, and pressure ulcers all spike on weekends. Source: CMS PBJ Daily Staffing (weekday vs weekend HPRD comparison)
↓ Lower turnover is better · Weekend staffing should match weekday staffing

Low COVID Vaccination Rates

Only 18.4% of residents and 34.7% of staff are up to date on COVID-19 vaccination. In a congregate care setting where outbreaks can be lethal, this is a serious gap in basic infection prevention.

COVID-19 Vaccination
Residents up to date on COVID-19 vaccine
18.4%vs25.3%
What this means: Only 18.4% of residents are up to date on their COVID-19 vaccination. National average is 25.3%. COVID remains the leading cause of infectious disease death in nursing homes. Vaccination dramatically reduces severe illness, hospitalization, and death — especially in the elderly. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_045)
Staff up to date on COVID-19 vaccine
34.7%vs41.3%
What this means: Only 34.7% of healthcare staff are up to date on COVID-19 vaccination. National average is 41.3%. Unvaccinated staff are more likely to bring COVID into the facility and transmit it to vulnerable residents. Staff vaccination rates are a proxy for infection control culture. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_041)
Flu & Pneumococcal — Long-Stay Residents
Seasonal influenza vaccine (long-stay)
88.2%vs95.5%
What this means: 88.2% of long-stay residents received the seasonal flu vaccine, below the 95.5% national average. Flu outbreaks in nursing homes can be fatal — the CDC estimates thousands of nursing home residents die from flu annually. Facilities should achieve near-universal vaccination. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 454)
Pneumococcal vaccine (long-stay)
86.1%vs93.4%
What this means: 86.1% of long-stay residents have received the pneumococcal vaccine. National average is 93.4%. Pneumonia is a leading cause of death in nursing home residents. The vaccine is a one-time shot — there's no logistical excuse for low rates. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 415)
Flu & Pneumococcal — Short-Stay Residents
Seasonal influenza vaccine (short-stay)
71.3%vs79.7%
What this means: 71.3% of short-stay residents received the flu vaccine during their stay. National average is 79.7%. Short-stay patients are often post-surgical and immunocompromised — making flu prevention especially important during their recovery. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 472)
Pneumococcal vaccine (short-stay)
74.5%vs81.7%
What this means: 74.5% of short-stay residents received the pneumococcal vaccine. National average is 81.7%. Many short-stay patients are elderly and at high risk for pneumonia — vaccination during a skilled nursing stay is a standard-of-care expectation. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 430)

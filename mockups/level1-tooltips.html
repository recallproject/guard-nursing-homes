📚 What do these staffing numbers actually mean?
▼

Nursing homes report their staffing to CMS through payroll records (called PBJ data). These numbers tell you how much direct nursing care each resident gets in a 24-hour period.

• RN HPRD of 0.42 means each resident gets about 25 minutes of Registered Nurse time per day. The national average is 40 minutes. Your facility is providing about 36% less RN care than average.
• 23 Zero-RN days means that for roughly 1 out of every 4 days last quarter, there was no RN on duty at all. If your family member had a sudden medical change on those days, the most qualified person available was an LPN.
• 78% RN turnover means that over the past year, more than 3 out of 4 RNs who worked here have left. Your family member is almost certainly being cared for by people who haven't been there long enough to know their medical history.

What families can do: Ask the facility directly — "How many RNs are on duty right now? How many were on duty last weekend?" If they can't or won't answer, that tells you something.

📚 What are deficiency tags and severity levels?
▼

When state inspectors visit a nursing home, they document problems as "deficiencies." Each deficiency has a severity level that tells you how serious it was:

● Immediate Jeopardy (IJ) — The most serious. A resident was in immediate danger of serious injury or death. This triggers urgent corrective action.
● Actual Harm — A resident was hurt, but not in immediate danger. This could be a fall that caused a fracture, a medication error that caused a reaction, or a pressure ulcer that developed from lack of repositioning.
● No Actual Harm, Potential for More Than Minimal Harm — Nobody was hurt yet, but inspectors found conditions that could lead to harm. Think of it as a warning sign.

Pattern vs. Isolated: "Pattern" means the problem was found across multiple residents or over time. "Isolated" means it was a one-time occurrence. A pattern of actual harm is much more concerning than an isolated deficiency.

Severity levels explained: CMS rates each deficiency on a scale from A (minor, no actual harm) to L (Immediate Jeopardy, imminent danger). "Serious Danger" = levels J/K/L, meaning inspectors found conditions likely to cause or already causing serious injury, harm, or death. "Residents Hurt" = levels G/H/I, meaning actual harm occurred but wasn't immediately life-threatening. "Minor" = levels D/E/F, potential for harm but no actual harm documented. Every citation is a finding from an unannounced on-site inspection by trained state surveyors.

05 How Are Residents Doing? New 7 CMS SOURCES

34 quality measures across 6 categories — drawn from MDS assessments, Medicare claims, SNF QRP, and VBP programs

High Chemical Sedation

28.3% of long-stay residents are on antipsychotics — nearly double the 16.7% national average. Combined with above-average sedative use, this suggests chemical restraints may be used in place of proper behavioral care.

Antipsychotic medication
28.3%vs16.7%
What this means: 28.3% of long-stay residents receive antipsychotic medications — drugs designed for schizophrenia and bipolar disorder but frequently used off-label to sedate dementia patients. The FDA has a black-box warning against antipsychotic use in elderly dementia patients due to increased risk of death. National average: 16.7%. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 419)
Anti-anxiety & sedative medication
24.1%vs19.6%
What this means: 24.1% of residents receive anti-anxiety or hypnotic medications. These include benzodiazepines (Xanax, Ativan, Valium) which increase fall risk in the elderly by 40–60%. National average: 19.6%. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 425)
Physical restraints
0.0%vs0.1%
What this means: No residents are physically restrained. This is good — physical restraints cause injury, pressure sores, and psychological trauma. Federal law requires facilities to be restraint-free except in documented emergencies. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 421)
Depressive symptoms
8.2%vs11.4%
What this means: 8.2% of residents show signs of depression. Below the 11.4% national average — though low numbers can reflect poor screening rather than good care. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 402)
Falls with major injury
4.2%vs3.3%
What this means: 4.2% of long-stay residents experienced a fall resulting in major injury (fractures, head trauma, dislocations). National average: 3.3%. High rates correlate with understaffing and sedative medication use. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 431)
↓ Lower is better for all measures in this category

Elevated Bedsore & UTI Rates

Above-average pressure ulcers and urinary tract infections — the two clearest indicators that residents aren't getting enough hands-on daily care.

Pressure ulcers (bedsores)
8.7%vs5.1%
What this means: 8.7% of long-stay residents developed new or worsening bedsores. National average: 5.1%. Bedsores are almost entirely preventable with proper repositioning every 2 hours — a direct reflection of staffing levels. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 403)
Urinary tract infections
3.4%vs1.7%
What this means: 3.4% of residents have UTIs — double the 1.7% national average. For elderly residents, UTIs can cause delirium, falls, and hospitalization. Often caused by delayed incontinence care or poor hygiene. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 424)
Catheter left in
0.8%vs1.0%
What this means: 0.8% of residents have an indwelling urinary catheter — below the 1.0% national average. Catheters should be removed as soon as medically possible; each additional day increases infection risk by 3–7%. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 409)
Significant weight loss
7.8%vs5.4%
What this means: 7.8% of residents experienced unintentional weight loss of 5%+ in 30 days (or 10% in 180 days). National average: 5.4%. Unexplained weight loss can indicate inadequate feeding assistance, depression, or neglect. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 426)
Ability to do daily tasks declined
18.2%vs14.9%
What this means: 18.2% of residents lost ability to perform basic ADLs (dressing, eating, bathing). National average: 14.9%. Functional decline accelerates when staff do tasks for residents instead of helping them maintain independence. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 406)
Walking ability worsened
19.1%vs16.3%
What this means: 19.1% of residents who could walk experienced a mobility decline. National average: 16.3%. Once a resident stops walking, recovery is rare. Facilities should actively maintain mobility through walking programs. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 405)
New/worsened incontinence
17.4%vs19.8%
What this means: 17.4% developed new or worsened bladder/bowel control — below the 19.8% national average. Incontinence management requires regular toileting schedules, a direct measure of staffing adequacy. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 407)
↓ Lower is better for all measures in this category

High Re-hospitalization Rate

23.9% of short-stay patients return to the hospital within 30 days — vs the 17.3% national average. If you're choosing this facility for post-surgery rehab, this is the most important number.

Re-hospitalized within 30 days
23.9%vs17.3%
What this means: 23.9% of patients admitted for short-term rehab end up back in the hospital within 30 days. This is a Medicare claims-based measure — actual hospital readmissions, not self-reported. National average: 17.3%. Source: Claims-Based Quality Measures (Code 471)
Emergency room visits
12.0%vs10.8%
What this means: 12.0% of short-stay residents visited the ER. National average: 10.8%. ER visits during a skilled nursing stay often indicate deterioration the facility couldn't manage on-site. Source: Claims-Based Quality Measures (Code 470)
Discharged to community
44.2%vs51.4%
What this means: 44.2% of short-stay patients returned to the community (home or assisted living). National average: 51.4%. Lower means fewer patients fully recover. Higher is better for this measure. Source: Claims-Based Quality Measures (Code 451)
Newly started on antipsychotics
3.8%vs1.6%
What this means: 3.8% of short-stay patients were newly put on antipsychotics they weren't taking before admission. National average: 1.6%. Starting antipsychotics during a rehab stay is a red flag for chemical sedation. Source: MDS Quality Measures (Code 452)
Healthcare-associated infections
0.094vs0.073
What this means: Risk-standardized infection rate of 0.094 vs the 0.073 national average. These are infections patients acquire during their stay — UTIs, pneumonia, wound infections — that the facility caused or failed to prevent. Source: Claims-Based Quality Measures (Code 486)
Medicare spending per beneficiary
1.14xvs1.0x avg
What this means: Medicare spends 14% more per patient here vs the national average. Higher spending often reflects unnecessary readmissions, longer stays, or preventable complications. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_006)
Functional improvement at discharge
88.7%vs94.3%
What this means: 88.7% of short-stay patients showed functional improvement at discharge. National average: 94.3%. If patients aren't improving, the facility isn't doing its job. Higher is better. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_007)
Falls with major injury (short-stay)
3.8%vs2.6%
What this means: 3.8% of short-stay patients experienced a fall resulting in major injury during their rehab stay. National average: 2.6%. Rehab patients are especially fall-prone due to post-surgical weakness and new medications. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_038)

The adjustment is based on hospital readmission rates — facilities that send patients back to the hospital too often get paid less. 78.6% of all nursing homes are penalized. Source: SNF VBP (Incentive Payment Multiplier)

High Staff Turnover & Weekend Staffing Drop

62.3% annual nursing staff turnover — most nurses leave within a year. Continuity of care suffers when the people caring for your loved one are constantly changing. Weekends see a -38% staffing drop vs weekdays.

Nursing staff turnover rate
62.3%vs47.7%
What this means: 62.3% of nursing staff (RNs, LPNs, CNAs) left in the last year. National average: 47.7%. Every 10% increase in turnover is associated with measurable declines in quality indicators. Source: SNF VBP (Provider Info)
Weekend staffing drop
-38%weekday → weekend
What this means: Staffing drops 38% on weekends compared to weekdays. Residents need the same care on Saturday as on Tuesday. Weekend cuts mean fewer people to answer call lights, administer medications, reposition bedridden residents, and respond to emergencies. Falls, missed medications, and bedsores all spike on weekends. Source: CMS PBJ Daily Staffing (weekday vs weekend HPRD comparison)
↓ Lower turnover is better  ·  Weekend staffing should match weekday staffing

Low COVID Vaccination Rates

Only 18.4% of residents and 34.7% of staff are up to date on COVID-19 vaccination. In a congregate care setting where outbreaks can be lethal, this is a serious gap in infection prevention.

COVID-19 Vaccination
Residents up to date on COVID-19
18.4%vs25.3%
What this means: Only 18.4% of residents are up to date on COVID-19. National average: 25.3%. COVID remains the leading cause of infectious disease death in nursing homes. Vaccination dramatically reduces severe illness and death in the elderly. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_045)
Staff up to date on COVID-19
34.7%vs41.3%
What this means: Only 34.7% of healthcare staff are up to date on COVID-19. National average: 41.3%. Unvaccinated staff are more likely to introduce COVID into the facility and transmit it to vulnerable residents. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_041)

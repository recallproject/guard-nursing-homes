High Antipsychotic Use

This facility medicates 28.3% of long-stay residents with antipsychotic drugs — nearly double the national average of 16.7% and in the worst 10% nationally (facilities above 29.6%). When combined with elevated anti-anxiety medication use, this suggests a pattern of chemical sedation rather than person-centered dementia care.

Antipsychotic medication
28.3%vs16.7% avg
What this means: 28.3% of long-stay residents receive antipsychotic medication without a qualifying diagnosis. CMS excludes residents with schizophrenia, Huntington's disease, and Tourette syndrome from this number. The national average is 16.7% (median 14.6%), and the worst 10% of facilities are above 29.6%. This facility is approaching that threshold. High rates are a strong signal of chemical restraint — using drugs to manage dementia behavior instead of providing appropriate care. Some antipsychotic use is clinically appropriate for severe agitation that poses safety risks, but rates this high warrant serious scrutiny.
Anti-anxiety & sedative medication
19.4%vs15.1% avg
What this means: 19.4% of long-stay residents receive anti-anxiety or hypnotic (sleeping) medication. Like antipsychotics, these drugs are sometimes used to sedate residents rather than address the underlying cause of their distress. Benzodiazepines significantly increase fall risk in elderly patients. When both antipsychotic AND anti-anxiety rates are elevated, it suggests a facility-wide pattern of relying on medication over care.
Physical restraints
0.0%vs0.4% avg
What this means: No residents are physically restrained (vests, mitts, belts, or side rails used as restraints). CMS has driven physical restraint use to near-zero nationally. Any facility above 1% should raise serious questions. Note: a facility can have zero physical restraints while still chemically restraining residents with antipsychotics and sedatives — look at both.
Depressive symptoms
3.8%vs4.1% avg
What this means: 3.8% of long-stay residents show moderate to severe depressive symptoms — near the national average. Depression is common in nursing home residents, especially those with dementia. A lower-than-average rate can indicate better screening and treatment — but very low rates at a facility with high sedation may indicate residents are too medicated to express distress, not that they aren't experiencing it.
Falls with major injury
4.2%vs3.2% avg
What this means: 4.2% of long-stay residents experienced a fall resulting in fracture, head injury, or other major harm. Residents with dementia are at higher fall risk — and ironically, so are residents on antipsychotic and anti-anxiety medications, which cause dizziness and impaired balance. A facility with high sedation rates AND high fall rates is a compounding red flag.

Elevated Bedsore & Infection Rates

This facility has above-average pressure ulcers and UTIs — two of the clearest indicators of understaffing. Bedsores happen when residents aren't being repositioned. UTIs happen when residents aren't getting basic hygiene care.

Pressure ulcers (bedsores)
8.7%vs5.2% avg
What this means: 8.7% of high-risk long-stay residents have stage 2 or worse pressure ulcers. Bedsores are largely preventable with proper repositioning, nutrition, and skin checks every 2 hours. A rate this high almost always means residents aren't being turned frequently enough — a direct consequence of not having enough nursing staff on the floor.
Urinary tract infections
4.8%vs3.1% avg
What this means: 4.8% of long-stay residents have a UTI. UTIs in nursing homes are largely preventable with adequate hydration and timely hygiene care. Elevated rates often mean residents aren't being changed frequently enough or aren't getting enough fluids — both require staff time that understaffed facilities don't have.
Catheter left in
1.2%vs1.8% avg
What this means: 1.2% of long-stay residents have an indwelling urinary catheter. Catheters dramatically increase infection risk and should only be used when medically necessary. Some facilities leave catheters in because it requires less staff time than assisting residents to the bathroom — convenience over safety.
Significant weight loss
9.1%vs7.1% avg
What this means: 9.1% of long-stay residents experienced significant unplanned weight loss. Dementia patients often forget to eat or have difficulty feeding themselves and need hands-on assistance. Elevated rates can indicate inadequate feeding help or nutritional monitoring. Very low rates could also mean the facility isn't weighing residents regularly enough to detect the problem.
Moderate or severe pain
6.4%vs8.3% avg
What this means: 6.4% of long-stay residents report moderate to severe pain. Good pain management is a basic quality indicator. However, very low rates in facilities with many dementia patients may indicate under-detection — residents with cognitive impairment often can't articulate that they're in pain.
Ability to do daily tasks declined
18.9%vs15.2% avg
What this means: 18.9% of long-stay residents lost the ability to perform daily activities (eating, bathing, dressing) they could previously manage. Some decline is expected with aging, but above-average rates suggest the facility isn't providing enough physical therapy or encouraging independence — which accelerates decline rather than slowing it.
Walking ability worsened
16.3%vs13.8% avg
What this means: 16.3% of long-stay residents who could previously walk lost that ability or needed significantly more help. Mobility loss in nursing homes is often preventable with regular physical therapy and walking programs. A facility that lets residents sit in wheelchairs all day will see high rates here — and once mobility is lost, it rarely comes back.

High Re-hospitalization Rate

This facility sends 24.1% of short-stay patients back to the hospital within 30 days — significantly above the national average. If you're choosing a facility for post-surgical rehab, this is the single most important number on the page.

Re-hospitalized within 30 days
24.1%vs17.3% avg
What this means: Nearly 1 in 4 short-stay patients end up back in the hospital within 30 days. This is the single most important metric for anyone choosing a rehab facility after surgery or a hospital stay. High rates indicate poor post-operative care, medication management failures, or inadequate clinical monitoring. Hospital discharge planners track this metric closely — and so should you.
Emergency room visits
14.2%vs10.8% avg
What this means: 14.2% of short-stay patients required an ER visit. Combined with high re-hospitalization, this means the facility struggles to manage post-acute complications on site. Strong facilities with adequate clinical staff can handle more issues in-house without sending patients back to the emergency room.
Newly started on antipsychotics
3.1%vs1.8% avg
What this means: 3.1% of short-stay patients were newly started on antipsychotic medication during their rehab stay. If your family member went in for a hip replacement and came out on an antipsychotic, that's a serious red flag. These drugs carry an FDA black-box warning for increased mortality in elderly patients and significantly increase fall risk — the opposite of what a rehab facility should be doing.

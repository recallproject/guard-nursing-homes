High Antipsychotic Use

This facility medicates 28.3% of long-stay residents with antipsychotic drugs — nearly double the national average of 16.7%. Combined with above-average sedative use, this suggests a pattern of chemical restraint rather than person-centered dementia care.

Antipsychotic medication
28.3%vs16.7% avg
What this means: 28.3% of long-stay residents receive antipsychotic medication without a qualifying diagnosis. CMS excludes residents with schizophrenia, Huntington's disease, and Tourette syndrome. The national median is 14.6%. The best 10% of facilities are at 5.1% or below. A rate this high places this facility among the worst in the country for potential chemical restraint.
Anti-anxiety & sedative medication
26.1%vs19.6% avg
What this means: 26.1% of long-stay residents receive anti-anxiety or hypnotic (sleeping) medication. These drugs increase fall risk significantly. When both antipsychotic AND sedative rates are elevated, it's a facility-wide pattern of using drugs instead of care.
Physical restraints
0.0%vs0.1% avg
What this means: No residents are physically restrained. CMS has driven restraint use to near-zero nationally (0.1% average). But a facility can have zero physical restraints while chemically restraining residents with drugs — look at the antipsychotic and sedative rates above.
Depressive symptoms
8.2%vs11.4% avg
What this means: 8.2% show moderate to severe depression — below the 11.4% national average. However, a lower rate at a facility with high sedation may mean residents are too medicated to express distress, not that they aren't experiencing it.
Falls with major injury
4.8%vs3.3% avg
What this means: 4.8% of long-stay residents fell and suffered fractures, head injuries, or other major harm. Antipsychotics and sedatives both increase fall risk — a facility with high medication rates AND high falls is a compounding red flag.

Elevated Bedsore & Infection Rates

Above-average pressure ulcers and UTIs — two of the clearest indicators of understaffing. Bedsores happen when residents aren't being repositioned. UTIs happen when residents aren't getting basic hygiene care.

Pressure ulcers (bedsores)
8.7%vs5.1% avg
What this means: 8.7% of high-risk long-stay residents have stage 2+ pressure ulcers. Bedsores are largely preventable with repositioning every 2 hours, adequate nutrition, and skin checks. A rate this high usually means residents aren't being turned often enough — a direct consequence of understaffing.
Urinary tract infections
3.4%vs1.7% avg
What this means: Double the national average for UTIs. UTIs in nursing homes are largely preventable with adequate hydration and timely hygiene care. In elderly patients, UTIs also cause acute confusion (delirium) that can be mistaken for worsening dementia.
Catheter left in
2.1%vs1.0% avg
What this means: Double the national average. Catheters dramatically increase infection risk and should only be used when medically necessary. Some facilities leave them in because it's easier than helping residents to the bathroom.
Significant weight loss
8.1%vs5.4% avg
What this means: 8.1% of residents have significant unplanned weight loss. Dementia patients often forget to eat or need hands-on feeding help. Above-average rates usually mean not enough staff to assist at mealtimes.
Ability to do daily tasks declined
19.2%vs14.9% avg
What this means: 19.2% of residents lost the ability to perform daily activities they could previously manage. Above-average rates suggest the facility isn't providing enough rehab or encouraging independence.
Walking ability worsened
21.4%vs16.3% avg
What this means: 21.4% of residents who could walk lost that ability or needed significantly more help. Once mobility is lost, it rarely comes back. A facility that lets residents sit in wheelchairs all day will see high rates here.
New/worsened incontinence
17.8%vs19.8% avg
What this means: 17.8% of residents developed new or worsened bowel/bladder incontinence — slightly below the national average. Regular toileting schedules and pelvic floor exercises can prevent or slow this decline.

High Re-hospitalization Rate

This facility sends 29.1% of short-stay patients back to the hospital within 30 days — well above the 23.9% national average. If you're choosing a facility for post-surgical rehab, this is the most important number on the page.

Re-hospitalized within 30 days
29.1%vs23.9% avg
What this means: Nearly 1 in 3 short-stay patients end up back in the hospital within 30 days. The national average is already high at 23.9%. This is the single most important metric for anyone choosing a rehab facility after surgery. High rates indicate poor post-operative care, medication errors, or inadequate clinical monitoring. Source: CMS Claims-Based Quality Measures
Emergency room visits
16.8%vs12.0% avg
What this means: 16.8% of short-stay patients required an ER visit. Strong facilities with adequate clinical staff handle more issues in-house without sending patients to the emergency room. Combined with high re-hospitalization, this facility struggles with post-acute complications. Source: CMS Claims-Based Quality Measures
Discharged to community
42.1%vs51.4% avg
What this means: Only 42.1% of short-stay patients successfully go home, compared to the national average of 51.4%. This is the outcome metric — it tells you whether people actually get better and leave, or whether they stay, get transferred, or decline. Higher is better here. Source: SNF QRP
Newly started on antipsychotics
3.2%vs1.6% avg
What this means: 3.2% of short-stay patients were newly started on antipsychotic medication during their rehab stay. If your family member went in for a hip replacement and came out on an antipsychotic, that's a serious red flag. These drugs carry an FDA black-box warning for increased mortality in elderly patients.
Healthcare-associated infections
9.8%vs7.3% avg
What this means: 9.8% of residents acquired infections requiring hospitalization during their stay at this facility. These are infections the patient didn't have when they arrived. Above-average rates signal infection control problems. Source: SNF VBP
Medicare spending per beneficiary
1.14vs1.0 avg
What this means: This facility costs Medicare 14% more per patient than the national average (1.0 = average). A score of 1.14 means Medicare spends $1.14 for every $1.00 spent at the typical facility. 51.9% of facilities nationwide are above average. Higher spending doesn't mean better care — it often means longer stays, more readmissions, or unnecessary services. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_006)
Functional improvement at discharge
88.7%vs94.3% avg
What this means: 88.7% of patients showed functional improvement (ability to eat, dress, walk, etc.) by the time they were discharged. The national average is 94.3%. This is a core measure of whether the rehab is actually working — if patients aren't improving, what's the point of the stay? Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_007)
Falls with major injury
3.8%vs2.6% avg
What this means: 3.8% of residents had falls resulting in major injury (fractures, head trauma, etc.). The national average is 2.6%. Falls are the leading cause of injury death in people over 65. High fall rates may indicate inadequate supervision, poor medication management, or unsafe environments. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_038)
CMS payment adjustment
-1.2%penalty
What this means: CMS reduced this facility's Medicare payments by 1.2% through the Value-Based Purchasing program. This penalty is based on rehospitalization rates — if too many patients bounce back to the hospital, the facility loses money. Nationally, 78.6% of facilities are penalized (multiplier below 1.0) and only 21.4% earn bonuses. A penalty means this facility performs worse than most on keeping patients out of the hospital. Source: SNF VBP (Incentive Payment Multiplier: 0.988)

High Staff Turnover

This facility has a 62.3% annual nursing staff turnover rate — meaning most of the nursing staff leaves within a year. Continuity of care suffers when the people caring for your loved one are constantly changing.

Nursing staff turnover rate
62.3%vs47.7% avg
What this means: 62.3% of nursing staff left this facility within a year. The national average is already a staggering 47.7%. The best 10% of facilities have turnover below 29.4%. High turnover means new, unfamiliar staff who don't know the residents — and it's a leading indicator of poor management and working conditions. CMS now tracks this as part of the Value-Based Purchasing program and adjusts Medicare payments accordingly. Source: SNF VBP
Weekend staffing drop
-38%weekday → weekend
What this means: Staffing drops 38% on weekends compared to weekdays — from 3.4 total nursing hours per resident per day to 2.1. Some facilities staff up on weekdays when inspectors might visit and run skeleton crews on weekends. CMS started publishing weekend staffing separately because of this pattern. If your loved one is there 7 days a week, weekend staffing matters. Source: PBJ Staffing + State Averages
↓ Lower turnover is better · Weekend staffing should match weekday staffing

Critically Low COVID Vaccination

Only 12.4% of residents at this facility are up to date on COVID-19 vaccination — less than half the already-low national average of 25.3%. Nursing home residents are among the most vulnerable populations. Flu and pneumococcal coverage is adequate but below benchmarks for long-stay residents.

COVID-19

Residents up to date on COVID-19 vaccine
12.4%vs25.3% avg
What this means: Only 12.4% of residents at this facility are up to date on their COVID-19 vaccination. The national average is just 25.3% — meaning most nursing homes are failing on this measure. Nearly a quarter of all facilities (2,305) have less than 10% of residents vaccinated. COVID-19 remains dangerous for elderly nursing home residents with compromised immune systems. Staff up to date on COVID-19 vaccine
28.9%vs41.3% avg
What this means: 28.9% of healthcare staff at this facility are up to date on COVID-19 vaccination, compared to the national average of 41.3%. Staff interact with dozens of residents daily and can carry infections between rooms. Low staff vaccination rates put residents — who may be too frail to mount a strong immune response even if vaccinated — at additional risk. Source: SNF QRP (Measure S_041)
